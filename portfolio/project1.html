CASE STUDY / TECHNICAL DEVELOPMENT

Seeds of Memories // Immersive Flower Field

2026 Interactive Environment Unity · C# · Shaders
CONCEPT & VISION

An immersive, two‑minute interactive experience where a player walks through a vast flower field, leaving behind a trail of colour. Ghost visitors – echoes of previous players – appear and interact, creating a growing, chaotic ecosystem of colour and memory. The world resets after each session, but the traces of past visitors linger.

MY ROLE: TECHNICAL DEVELOPMENT & SYSTEM INTEGRATION
01

Integrating Two Prototypes

Merged separate interaction systems from earlier prototypes into one cohesive Unity project. Resolved conflicts between colour‑trail mechanics, visitor spawning, and environmental behaviours.

Flower Shader Optimisation

Ghost Visitor System

Dynamic Flower Growth

Created a system where flowers grow outside the original field based on player movement trail. Added a short delay before enabling colliders to prevent unintended spawning inside the field.

CHALLENGES & SOLUTIONS

Performance Issues

Temporarily removed flower swaying, bending, glow effects, and fireflies to reduce lag. Prioritised stability over visual polish.

Unclear Visitor Interaction

Players didn't understand that touching visitors activated new ones. Added colour highlights to newly spawned visitors, which faded after interaction to signal activation.

Restricted Field Boundaries

Players felt boxed in by the square boundary. Redesigned the field as a circular area with natural fade‑out using sphere colliders and density falloff.

Version Control Woes

Repeated file‑zipping caused conflicts. Set up a shared GitHub repository to track changes and avoid overwriting work.

FINAL REFLECTION

Looking back, I'm shocked by how much development became a constant negotiation between ambition and technical stability. Integrating two prototypes was harder than I expected – every new feature risked breaking something else. But removing the glow effects and fireflies was the right call; stability first. The ghost visitor system taught me to stop assuming players will just "get it". Watching real people play revealed gaps I'd never have seen alone. If I were doing this again, I'd start version control on day one – GitHub saved us hours of headaches. The ending zoom‑out sequence gave the experience a clear finish. Overall, I'm proud of what we built, even if the path was messier than I expected.
